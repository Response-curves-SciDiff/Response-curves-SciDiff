TL;DR

We measure, for each noise level, the largest deterministic DDIM jump a trained scientific diffusion sampler can take while keeping the endpoint spectrum faithful to a fine-step reference. The resulting response curve is a calibrated local safe-step budget: it predicts unsafe held-out steps, conservatively audits multi-step schedules, and falsifies both the single-power-law and strict-CFL stories.

Abstract

Scientific diffusion samplers are often discretized with generic noise grids borrowed from image generation. For PDE emulators, this is risky: a coarse sampler can produce visually plausible fields while silently violating spectral diagnostics. We introduce response curves, a censoring-aware evaluation protocol that measures, at each noise level, the largest deterministic DDIM jump whose endpoint spectrum matches a fine-step deterministic reference. The curve acts as a local safe-step budget, not just a descriptive fit: budgets calibrated from ACDM/Iso turbulence checkpoints predict fresh held-out initial conditions, with below-budget pass rates 100%, at-budget pass rates 90.6–97.9%, and 1.2×-budget pass rates collapsing to 1.8–20.1%. At the schedule level, the same budget audit conservatively flags all median-failing held-out multi-step stress schedules, with false positives near the threshold.

The evaluation also falsifies two common intuitions. The response curve is not a single global power law: across six threshold-by-seed runs it is reproducibly two-regime, with low-noise exponent 0.656 ± 0.050 and high-noise exponent 1.046 ± 0.046, while a 4/3 law is rejected in every run. Nor is the limit explained by a strict first-order CFL-style rule under our update-field stiffness proxy: Δσmax ∝ λmax−1 fails. Mechanism probes instead support an accuracy-limited model e(h, σ) ≈ C(σ) hp(σ), where stiffness-like gains and denoiser saturation modulate the local error coefficient and regime transition. The result is a deployment-time evaluation methodology for scientific diffusion samplers: response curves predict unsafe local steps, conservatively audit deployment schedules, and reveal when appealing physical step-size laws are the wrong explanation.

The Response Law Is Two-Regime, Not 4/3

Across six runs — thresholds τ ∈ {0.10, 0.20, 0.30} crossed with two ACDM/Iso checkpoint seeds — the response curve is reproducibly two-regime. Low-noise slopes concentrate near 2/3, high-noise slopes near 1, and a single power-law fit is decisively rejected (ΔAIC ≥ 47 in every run). The hypothesized 4/3 Kolmogorov-style scaling is not supported.

Multi-threshold validation. The response curve is reproducibly two-regime; 0/2737 monotonicity violations, min ΔAIC 47.4 against a single power, max single-run exponent 1.135.

Takeaway. The step-size envelope is not a single global exponent — it has a regime transition co-located with a denoiser-saturation crossing.

Calibrated Budgets Predict Safe Local Steps

Budgets calibrated from the response curve transfer to fresh held-out initial conditions. Below-budget steps pass uniformly; at budget, pass rates remain high (90.6–97.9%); just 1.2× the budget collapses pass rates to 1.8–20.1%.

Held-out safe-step prediction. The calibrated budget sharply separates safe and unsafe local jumps on fresh ICs.

Schedule-Level Failure Audit

We audit complete coarse schedules by checking whether any local jump exceeds the measured response envelope. Across 120 stress schedule groups (NFE ∈ {2, 3, 4, 6, 8}), the audit flags every median-failing group (20 TP, 0 FN) with 10 conservative false positives near the threshold. Spearman correlation with spectrum error is 0.894 and with state error is 0.980 at the instance level.

Schedule audit. Response-budget overshoot Rmax > 1 conservatively flags every median-failing schedule on held-out ICs.

Contributions

  • C1. A censoring-aware protocol for measuring local safe-step budgets Δσmax(σ) in trained scientific diffusion samplers.
  • C2. Calibrated budgets transfer to held-out ICs and audit multi-step deployment schedules with conservative (zero-FN) failure flagging.
  • C3. The response law is reproducibly two-regime, not a single power; the 4/3 Kolmogorov-style intuition is rejected on ACDM/Iso checkpoints.
  • C4. Strict first-order CFL fails; an accuracy-limited error model e(h, σ) ≈ C(σ) hp(σ) better organizes stiffness, saturation, and local-error probes.
  • C5. Cross-variant checks (NCN/Iso, Inc, Tra, RefinerStepper) frame response curves as an evaluation methodology, not a universal architecture-independent law.
